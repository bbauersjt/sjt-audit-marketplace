# Financial Statement Narrative Voice

The written parts of a financial statement set: notes and disclosures, accounting policy notes, MD&A-type narrative, and the schedule of findings and responses. Covers all three regimes: commercial (FASB ASC), nonprofit (ASC 958), and governmental (GASB). The findings sub-section at the bottom covers the Single Audit schedule of findings and questioned costs.

Layout, pagination, continuation headings, and table structure belong to the docx, fix-page-breaks, and FS-review skills. This module is wording only. Never rewrite prescribed text: the auditor's report and opinion, required statement titles, and any GAAP, GASB, GAGAS, or Uniform Guidance language mandated verbatim stay in their exact form.

## Register vs. the shared baseline
Formal, precise, complete. This is the one module where full sentences and standard boilerplate are correct, because a disclosure has to stand on its own and satisfy the standard. The baseline still governs: plain, no marketing words, no drama, no selling, no em dashes in your prose. The tension the reader named: concise and referring to standards, but not bare minimum, and not elaborating on anything a professional would already infer.

## The reader
A knowledgeable user of financial statements, a reviewer, and a peer reviewer. They know the standards. State the entity's policy and the entity-specific facts. Do not teach the standard behind the policy. "The Company amortizes goodwill on a straight-line basis over ten years" is the disclosure; how goodwill amortization works is not.

## Name the thing first
Open every note and every policy with its subject as the first words, then state the policy. "Accounts receivable consists of amounts due from clients..." "Goodwill represents the excess of purchase price over fair value..." "Prepaid expenses include outlays for services that will be received in the future." "Class A units carry full voting rights..." The caption names the topic; the first sentence names it again as the grammatical subject and defines it.

## Referring to standards
State the reporting framework once, up front: "in accordance with accounting principles generally accepted in the United States of America (U.S. GAAP)," adding "applicable to government units" and the GASB reference for governmental, the FASB ASC reference for nonprofit.

Cite a specific standard by its number where it drives the policy, not everywhere. Commercial and nonprofit: "Accounting Standards Codification (ASC) 350-20," "ASC 606," "ASC 450." Governmental: "GASB Statement No. 54," "GASB Statement No. 101." Cite the standard that governs the treatment being disclosed; do not string-cite for authority a professional doesn't need.

## Italics
Italics carry a convention here, so apply it deliberately:

- Titles of standards and publications: the codification title after the number (ASC 350-20: _Goodwill_; ASC 606: _Revenue from Contracts with Customers_; ASC 450 _Accounting for Contingencies_), and named standards referenced by title (_Government Auditing Standards_). GASB statement titles in these sets run plain after the number (GASB Statement No. 101, Compensated Absences); match the set you're in.
- Run-in labels and defined-term headings that begin a classification: the net asset classes (_Net Assets without Donor Restrictions_), the GASB 54 fund balance classes (_Nonspendable_, _Restricted_, _Committed_, _Assigned_, _Unassigned_), and the schedule sub-headers (_Federal awards_, _Nonfederal awards_).
- In the findings schedule, the labeled parts of each finding (_Criteria_, _Condition_, _Cause and Effect_, _Auditor's Recommendation_, _Management's Response_, _Current Status_).

## Defined terms
Name in full on first use, give the short form in parentheses, then use the short form. Legal-style defined terms take quotes (the "Company," the "Nation," the "Credit Agreement"); acronyms don't (PNNMEF, FDIC, IHS, SWM). Define once, then be consistent.

## Concision and level
State the policy and the entity's facts, then stop. Standard boilerplate that the standard requires (use of estimates, subsequent events) stays, kept tight. Everything else earns its place: if a professional reader would infer it, cut it. Don't pad a disclosure to look thorough, and don't strip a required disclosure to a fragment. The SEC-examination disclosure and the supplemental-interest disclosure in the UCAIG set are the right depth: the facts, the amounts, the effect, and management's position, no narration.

## Cross-references
Point rather than repeat. "(see also Note 4)," "See Note 5," "as described above," "described in Note 7," "as discussed in Note 11." Disclose a fact once in its home note and reference it elsewhere.

## Numbers and dates
Figures to the dollar. "Approximately" only for a genuine estimate ("approximately $3.9 billion"). Prior-year comparatives with "respectively." Full dates. State the subsequent-events evaluation date and that it is the date the statements were available to be issued.

## New pronouncements
Name the statement and title, state what it changed in one or two sentences, then state its effect on this entity plainly. "Because the Grants Fund does not carry a compensated absences liability, this standard had no impact."

# Findings and responses (Single Audit schedule)

## Structure of one finding
Number and title on one line: year-sequence, area, short descriptive title. "2025-001 - Payroll - Documentation of Authorized Pay Rates." Then the labeled parts, each an italic run-in label:

- _Criteria_: what should be. The control, policy, or requirement, stated as the standard the condition is measured against. "Internal controls over payroll should ensure that employee pay rates are properly authorized..."
- _Condition_: what was found, with the population. Lead with the test. "For 25 payroll transactions selected for testing, the Grants Fund could not produce..." List multiple exceptions as bullets.
- _Cause and Effect_: why it happened, then the risk it creates. State mitigating factors where they exist, labeled and enumerated. "This risk is mitigated by (a)..., (b)..., and (c)...."
- _Auditor's Recommendation_: what to do, direct. "The Nation should maintain a personnel action form... and retain it in personnel records..."
- _Management's Response_: management's voice, drafted for them. Open with the posture ("Management agrees with the finding."), then the concrete corrective steps and the going-forward commitment.

## Summary of auditor's results (Section I)
Prescribed yes/no format. Fill the answers, don't reword the questions.

## Prior-year findings
_Condition_ restated briefly, then _Current Status_ with the disposition in the standard terms: Corrected, or Repeated and modified (and where it now appears). "Repeated and modified. The condition recurred in modified form and is reported as current-year finding 2025-002."

## What "done" looks like
A reviewer reads a note and can tie it to the balance and the standard without a question. A finding gives criteria, condition with the sample, cause, effect, recommendation, and response, each in its place, in the firm's standard terms, with nothing narrated.
