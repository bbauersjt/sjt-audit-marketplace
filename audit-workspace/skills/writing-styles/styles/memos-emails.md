# Memo and Email Voice

Two deliverables share this file because they route from the same menu row, but they are not one voice. Emails are casual, in the register you'd use with a teammate. Technical and position memos are formal and run close to the workpaper voice. Pick the half that matches.

- Email voice: casual register, for internal and external messages.
- Memo voice: formal register, for technical/position memos and memos of record.

---

# Email voice

## Register vs. the shared baseline
This is the one place the SKILL.md shared baseline bends. The baseline is tuned cold and terse for workpapers. Email is casual and social. So:

- Contractions are the default, including loose ones ("gonna," "kind of," "wanna").
- Moderate hedging is correct, not a flaw: "I think," "probably," "I'd recommend." This is social softening, not vagueness. Still state facts, numbers, and dates plainly.
- The baseline rules that DO carry over: no em dashes (your own tell), no marketing words, no throat-clearing, plain not produced, say it once.

## The reader
A colleague or a client who knows the context. Write like you're continuing a conversation, not opening a file. Give the substance and the next step, then stop.

## Length and shape
One short paragraph is the default. Structure follows audience:

- Internal, to a colleague: often a single line. Acknowledgment paired with the next action ("Here you go." / "Now I got them. Thanks!"). No greeting or sign-off needed on a quick reply.
- External or client: expand. Greeting, a brief context line (often noting a delay, a short week, or a recent vacation), the substance, then sign-off.

## Openings
- Greeting: "Hi [Name]," is the default. Rotate with "Hey [Name],".
- If late, lead with the apology as the opener: "Sorry for the delay -" or a context line ("Sorry I didn't get back with you last week, I forgot we had a real short week").

## Closings
- "Thanks, Brett" / "Thanks," on its own line above the name / "Thank you," / "Let me know."
- First contact or fee discussion gets a slightly rounder close ("Let me know on it").
- The full CPA signature block appends automatically. Don't write one.

## Pronouns and ownership
Default to "I" for things you own ("I'd recommend," "I forgot," "I didn't get back"). "We" for firm work in progress ("We're getting close to done," "we had to go through the full review process").

## Bullets vs. prose
Bullets only when listing three or more items. Otherwise prose. Technical walkthroughs to colleagues run as prose paragraphs with the steps embedded, not as bullets ("If you go to Suralink and log in, there will be a link next to clients called 'secure file sharing'. If you click that...").

## Questions
Batch them and label them rather than scattering them through the body. "Couple of questions:" then separate or numbered lines, placed before the sign-off.

## Punctuation
- No em dashes. Use a spaced hyphen between clauses for quick context: "Here's a draft - we had to go through the full review process so it took a bit."
- Parentheses for caveats and asides: "(this is for awards, not contracts)".
- Numbers, dollar figures, and dates stated plainly inline.

## Timing and next steps
State timing in one sentence, plainly, with the honest range: "Tomorrow by COB, or at the worst Monday morning first thing." Fold constraints into the same line when they explain the timing ("...but Armando can only do Thursday, so if it's Wednesday it's just me").

## Example bank
Match the shape, not the specifics.

- Internal one-liner: "Here you go."
- Scheduling reply, external, casual: "Hi Kelly, Had to get with Armando - can we do Thursday? Thanks, Brett"
- Timing line with the constraint baked in: "Any time in the Thursday window, or any time Wednesday but Armando can only do Thursday, so if it's Wednesday it's just me."
- Apology + context + ask, to a client: "Hi Michael, These work. We're getting close to done. Sorry I didn't get back with you last week I forgot we had a real short week... What's a good time for you all?"
- Instructional walkthrough, internal, prose: "Rahul, If you go to Suralink and log in, there will be a link next to clients called 'secure file sharing'. If you click that, there's a couple options under it - click 'my link' and it will give you a link you can copy."
- Team-wide logistics: "Handout for training tomorrow. Please unzip this in your documents or wherever is a good place to stash it. Open up index.html in the main folder, and bookmark it in your browser. Thanks,"

## What "done" looks like
It reads like you dashed it off in thirty seconds and it happens to be complete: the answer, the next step, and the timing, in as few lines as that takes. If it reads polished or produced, it's wrong.

# Memo voice

## Register vs. the shared baseline
Formal. Runs close to styles/workpapers.md (sourced, terse, decision-oriented) but with more connective prose, because a memo argues a position or documents a judgment rather than logging a test. The full shared baseline applies: plain, no marketing words, no em dashes, say it once. Unlike the email half, no casual register and no loose contractions.

## The reader
A reviewer or peer reviewer who will rely on the memo to understand a judgment call and re-reach it. Give the conclusion, the facts that drive it, the authority, and the disposition. Assume they know the standard. Cite it, don't teach it.

## The arc
Every memo follows one arc: conclusion or purpose up front, then the sourced facts, then the reasoning, then the disposition. Header block (entity / memo title / date MM/DD/YYYY) sits above the body; its layout belongs to the docx skill. Keep the title a plain noun phrase naming the subject ("Delinquent Contributions Presentation Memo," "Inventory Turnover Analytics").

## Openers (pick by memo type)
- Position / technical conclusion: state the conclusion in the first sentence and say what it rests on. "We copied the following ... to justify our conclusion that [conclusion]."
- Memo of record (documenting a call or inquiry): lead with the event, dated, naming who did it in full third person. "On [date], [Preparer] spoke with [Contact], [Title] for [Organization]..." This is a record, so the full name and date carry weight.
- Analytics / explanatory: "The purpose of this memo is to [describe X]."

## Sourcing
Same tokens as workpapers. "Per [source]" for facts (per Scott, per Aaron's notes, per management), cross-reference with "See [index]" or "at [ref]" (Per O-2-1, See 10 series). Name a person with their role, and for an inquiry the date. Figures plain and inline ($150,000), dates MM/DD/YYYY.

## Citing authority
Cite the standard by section, not by explaining the concept. "From ASC 805-30," "Schedule H, Part IV, Line 4a." If the codification text or research lives in its own workpaper, reference it rather than reproducing it: "per research at [ref]." Reproduce excerpts only when the memo is where they live, and keep clear which words are quoted and which are yours.

## Reasoning
Explicit because/therefore chains. State the driver, then the result. "Because the contingency is estimable, and for a set period, we will include the contingent liability in the purchase price." "Because of this, all sales are 100% profit ... Therefore, traditional analytics relying on COGS ... would be ineffective."

## Conclusion and disposition
End on what the firm will do or what follows, not a restatement. A decision ("we should show them as corrected with the 2023 report and not report them in 2024"), a forward-looking note ("Will be necessary for 2026 and beyond"), or a pointer to the testwork ("See 10 series for our tests over donated goods revenue.").

## Pronouns
"We / our" for firm positions and decisions: our conclusion, we will include, we should show, our tests. Full-name third person when the memo records a specific person's action for the file.

## Example skeletons

- Position memo: "[Conclusion, stated as the reason for the memo]. Per [person or schedule], [client facts, with figures]. Per [ref], [corroboration]. [Standard cite, or per research at ref]. Because [driver], we will [treatment]. [Forward-looking note]."
- Memo of record: "On [date], [Full Name] spoke with [Name], [title] for [org]. Per [Name], [the guidance obtained]. [Client's facts]. Because [reason], we should [action]."
- Analytics memo: "The purpose of this memo is to [X]. [Client facts explaining why the normal metric doesn't apply]. Because of this, [result]. Therefore, [conclusion]. See [ref] for [related testwork]."

## What "done" looks like
The reviewer reads it once and knows the judgment and why: conclusion, driving facts with sources, authority, disposition. Nothing explaining a standard they already know, no restatement of the conclusion at the end.
