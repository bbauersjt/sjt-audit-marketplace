# Client and Staff Facing Voice

Two sub-registers share this file, split by audience:

- Presentations to a client or a governing body (audit results decks, board or council presentations).
- Training and resource docs for staff (how-to handouts, internal reference).

Layout, slide masters, and file mechanics belong to the pptx and docx skills. This module is wording only. Never restate prescribed report or opinion language; on a slide you summarize the opinion in plain terms, you do not reproduce the auditor's report.

## Register vs. the shared baseline
The shared baseline holds (plain, no marketing words, no em dashes, say it once), with one addition unique to this module: for a lay audience you actively translate. Where workpapers assume an expert reader, a board or council may not be accountants, so audit terms get rendered as outcomes. This is the one place where saying a bit more, in plainer words, is correct.

# Presentations (client / governing body)

## The reader
Often not accountants. A tribal council, a board, a finance committee. They want to know what you did, whether the news is good, and what it means. Give them the result first and the mechanics only as far as they need.

## Two versions, tailored to the room
Keep a technical version and a lay version of the same deck when the audience differs. A technical deck and a council deck for the same audit show the split:

- Technical deck: names the standards (GASB 101, Government Auditing Standards, Uniform Guidance), uses "unmodified opinion," keeps the full required-communications slides.
- Council version: translates each of those into a plain outcome, drops what a lay board doesn't act on, and leads with the good news.

Concrete translations, technical to lay:
- "Unmodified opinion" becomes "best opinion possible - unmodified."
- "Required Communications to the Tribal Council" (accounting policies, estimates, AJEs) becomes "Significant Activity This Year," carrying only what a board cares about (the $7.9M and $5.6M projects).
- The internal-control paragraph becomes "Evaluate and test controls / Internal processes to prevent and detect fraud and errors."
- Separate current-year and prior-year findings slides collapse into one "Audit Results - No Findings."
- Add a plain "Procedures We Perform" slide the technical deck doesn't need ("Test over 100 expenditures," "Verification of cash, investments").

## Lead with the result
Put the answer first and frame it as reassurance when it's clean. "All three opinions clean, and no findings." Then the detail. Don't make a board read to the end to learn the news was good.

## Slide titles
A plain noun phrase naming the slide's topic or its takeaway. "Purpose of Our Audit," "Auditor's Reports," "Significant Activity This Year," "Audit Results - No Findings." When the result is the point, put it in the title.

## Bullets
Fragments, parallel in form, one idea each. Verb-led for actions ("Test over 100 expenditures," "Evaluate and test controls"), noun-led for topics ("Depreciation," "Allowance for doubtful accounts"). No sentences where a fragment carries it.

## Numbers and references
Plain and inline: $7.9 million, $21,435,012. Program names carry their number in parentheses (Indian Self-Determination (93.441)). Round to the level the audience needs ($7.9 million on a board slide, the exact figure only where it's the point).

## Glossing a term
When a term has to appear, gloss it in plain words in a parenthetical rather than assuming it lands: "Highest level of assurance a CPA can provide (and the best opinion you can receive)."

## What "done" looks like
A board member reads the slide and knows the outcome and what it means without a CPA translating it for them. Result first, plain words, only the detail they'd act on.

# Training and resource docs (staff facing)

This sub-register also governs skill documentation and internal how-to or reference write-ups.

## Sterile, like a manual
Write it like instructions for assembling a chair. Sterile, step by step, no voice. No warm-up, no framing, no "this powerful feature lets you," no narrating why the step matters. Just the steps, in order. The one carve-out is for skill documentation: include explanation only where the model or reader needs that context to perform the task correctly, not to make the page read better. If a line isn't load-bearing for doing the task, cut it.

## The reader
A staff member doing the task while they read. They want the click-path, in order, with the exact labels, and a heads-up on anything that won't be obvious. Assume firm and software familiarity at a professional level; don't explain why, explain how. No overexplaining.

## Steps
Imperative, verb-first, one action per step, in order. "Open the engagement's Trial Balance." "Click Add Accounts in the right rail." "Enter an Account number and Account name, then click Add." No subject, no throat-clearing.

## Name the interface exactly
Use the software's own labels verbatim, capitalized as the software has them: Add New dialog, the Type dropdown, the Group cell, Create New Engagement. A reader hunting for a button needs the real word on it. Use an arrow for a navigation path: "Create New Engagement from Template -> Create New Engagement."

## Gloss lines
Under a heading or a download, one short fragment saying what it is. "Folder index for a new engagement binder." "Two-column lookup: ABBREV, DESCRIPTION." Enough to pick the right one, no more.

## Notes and gotchas
Call out what the screen won't tell them: default states, what maps automatically, what has to happen first. "Group, fund, type, and class fields show Unassigned until set." "The account type maps automatically from the code, it is not a required column." "Fund types and funds (2 layers) - required before a fund TB import." Write these only where the reader would otherwise trip.

## What "done" looks like
Someone follows it start to finish and completes the task without asking a question, because every label is the real one, the order is right, and the one non-obvious thing was flagged.
